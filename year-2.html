BBA Curriculum

Year Two: Building Functional Expertise and Financial Depth

The second year of the BBA Finance program marks a critical inflection point, transitioning students from foundational principles to a sophisticated exploration of core functional areas and their intersection with finance.

Organizational Behaviour

This course delves into the study of human dynamics within an organizational context, examining the interplay between individuals, groups, and the organizational structure itself. It's crucial for understanding human factors in financial successes and failures.

Curriculum Index:

  • The Individual: Foundations of individual behaviour (attitudes, emotions), personality, values, perception, and individual decision-making.
  • Motivation Concepts: Early theories (Maslow, Herzberg, McGregor) and contemporary theories (Self-Determination, Goal-Setting, Equity Theory).
  • The Group: Foundations of group behaviour, understanding work teams, communication processes, and leadership theories (Trait, Behavioral, Contingency, Charismatic, Transformational).
  • Power, Politics, and Conflict: Bases of power, organizational politics, the conflict process, and negotiation strategies.
  • The Organization System: Foundations of organization structure (design elements, common designs), organizational culture, and managing organizational change and development.

Financial Management

This pivotal course formalizes the student's transition to a finance-focused professional, applying accounting principles and mathematical tools to investment, financing, and dividend decisions with the objective of maximizing shareholder wealth.

Curriculum Index:

  • Foundational Concepts: Nature, scope, and objectives of financial management, functions of a finance manager, time value of money (compounding, discounting), valuation of securities (bond and equity), and risk and return (CAPM).
  • Capital Budgeting (Investment Decisions): Traditional methods (payback period, ARR) and discounted cash flow (NPV, IRR, PI) methods, estimation of project cash flows, and risk analysis in capital budgeting.
  • Capital Structure & Cost of Capital (Financing Decisions): Component costs of capital (debt, preference, equity), Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), leverage analysis (operating, financial, combined), and capital structure theories (NI, NOI, MM approach).
  • Working Capital Management: Concepts and determinants of working capital, operating cycle, cash and liquidity management, credit (receivables) management, and inventory management (EOQ, reorder point).

Marketing Management

Presented as the commercial engine driving revenue, this course provides the framework to evaluate sales forecasts, assess product viability, and appreciate intangible assets like brand equity, all critical for finance professionals.

Curriculum Index:

  • Understanding Marketing & Capturing Insights: Defining marketing, developing marketing strategies and plans, and marketing research & forecasting.
  • Connecting with Customers: Analyzing consumer markets (influencing factors, buying decision process), analyzing business markets, and identifying market segments and targets.
  • Building Strong Brands & Shaping the Market (Product): Creating brand equity & positioning, competitive dynamics (identifying, analyzing, competitive strategies), and setting product strategy (characteristics, classifications, life-cycle).
  • Delivering & Communicating Value (Price, Place, Promotion): Developing pricing strategies and programs, designing and managing marketing channels (place), and managing integrated marketing communications (promotion).

Human Resource Management

This course covers the principles of strategically managing an organization's most valuable asset: its people. It's vital for analyzing cost structures, operational efficiency, and talent retention.

Curriculum Index:

  • Introduction to HRM: Conceptual framework, nature, scope, objectives, functions, and the role of the HR manager, including policy formulation.
  • Acquisition of Human Resources: Human Resource Planning (HRP) objectives and process, job analysis and design, and recruitment and selection processes.
  • Development of Human Resources: Placement, induction, socialization, training and development (needs assessment, methods, evaluation), and executive development.
  • Maintenance and Compensation: Performance appraisal (objectives, process, methods), compensation management (objectives, components, factors), and employee relations (grievance handling, industrial disputes, discipline, HRIS).

Macroeconomics

This subject provides the indispensable context for all financial decisions by examining the economy as a whole, including key macroeconomic variables and the policy tools used to manage them.

Curriculum Index:

  • Introduction & National Income: Nature and scope of macroeconomics, key macroeconomic issues, circular flow of income, and national income accounting concepts (GDP, GNP, NNP).
  • Theory of Income & Employment: Classical and Keynesian models, components of aggregate demand (consumption, investment), and the theory of the multiplier.
  • Money, Banking & Prices: Functions of money, credit creation, central bank functions, demand for and supply of money (Quantity Theory, Liquidity Preference Theory).
  • Inflation, Unemployment & Policy: Meaning, types, causes, and effects of inflation, the Phillips Curve, business cycles, and macroeconomic policy (Monetary and Fiscal policies).
  • Open Economy Macroeconomics: International trade and finance, Balance of Payments, and foreign exchange rates.

Business Statistics

Building on business mathematics, this course introduces uncertainty, providing the framework for inferences, predictions, and data-driven decisions based on incomplete or variable data—essential for financial analysis.

Curriculum Index:

  • Probability & Distributions: Basic concepts of probability, rules of probability (addition, multiplication, Bayes' Theorem), and probability distributions (Binomial, Poisson, Normal).
  • Sampling & Statistical Inference: Population and sample, methods of sampling, sampling distributions, Central Limit Theorem, point and interval estimation, and hypothesis testing (Z-test).
  • Advanced Inference: Small sample tests (t-distribution, t-test), Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Test (Goodness of Fit, Independence of Attributes), and Analysis of Variance (ANOVA).
  • Bivariate Analysis: Correlation analysis (concept, types, methods like Karl Pearson, Spearman's Rank Correlation) and Regression analysis (concept, Least Squares, fitting regression lines, Multiple Regression introduction).
